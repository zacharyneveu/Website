In my opinion, four features are likely to make an audio codec useful and popular. A good
codec should be efﬁcient, taking only a small amount of space to store a large ﬁle. It should also
be able to encode this ﬁle quickly, and decode it quickly using minimal computing resources. The
encoded ﬁles from a good codec should be compatible with a wide range of different music
players, and should be obtainable from a range of different sources. Finally, a good codec should
produce music that sounds much like the original recording. By comparing todays codecs by
these standards, one can determine which codecs are most useful and likely to become popular.
Findings
The efﬁciency in ﬁle size of a codec is a critical factor in the usefulness of the codec.
Lossless and Lossy encoding were both invented as a means to shrink ﬁle sizes for audio and this
remains their primary goal. According to Hal Robertson, the maximum compression achieved by
lossless encoding is a ﬁle size 30% to 50% the size of the original uncompressed audio ﬁle (59).
Different lossless encoders have different compression ratios. Norbert Nowak and Wojciech
Zabierowski have found that the Monkey’s Audio (APE) format is the most efﬁcient popular
lossless format though FLAC is right behind it (434). According to hydrogenaudio, a knowledge
base for audio enthusiasts, the Apple lossless format, ALAC, has low efﬁciency for a lossless
encoding format. In general, lossy codecs are far more efﬁcient than lossless ones. While lossless
formats can in some cases achieve compression ratios of 3 to 1 (33% of original size), Nowak and
Zabierowski’s test of lossy audio codecs shows that it is not uncommon to have lossy
compression ratios of 10 or even 20 to 1 (434). One important thing to note about lossy
compression, however is that ﬁle sizes can be made tiny, but this comes at the cost of sound
quality. Nowak and Zabierowski found that when compressed to 320kbps, most lossy formats
have compression ratios of just under 23% (434). At 128kbps, these formats have compression
ratios of around 9% (Nowak and Zabierowski 434). Inherently, because they delete certain
material, lossy codecs excel at storing music at small data rates.
