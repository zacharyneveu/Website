Lossless encoding is a method of storing audio which preserves the entirety of the data
from the uncompressed signal and stores it in a way that takes up less space. According to Mat
Hans and Ronald Schafer, the authors of
Lossless Compression of Digital Audio,
this is done in
four steps. First, they say, the left and right audio channels are combined into one ”mid” channel
and one ”side” channel. The mid channel stores the sum of the left and right channels while the
side channel stores their difference (22). Second, audio is broken up into groups of samples called
”windows” (Hans and Schafer 22). Third, a function is found for each window that can predict
what the next sample is based on the last one (Hans and Schafer 22). Finally, they say that the
functions for the mid and side channels are stored using a technique called ”entropy coding”
which eliminates redundancies in the data (30). By eliminating redundancy, lossless compression
can reduce the size of the original uncompressed ﬁle, while retaining the exact same set of
information. As with uncompressed audio, there are several choices for lossless audio codecs.
Apple supports the ALAC format. FLAC is a very popular open source format supported by the
Xiph foundation. Monkey’s Audio (APE) is another open source codec that has gained popularity.
In contrast to lossless audio codecs, lossy codecs do not preserve the entirety of the
original data. Lossy encoding makes use of the fact that the human ear and brain limit the sounds
that we hear and notice in music, deleting sounds that are not heard or not noticed by the listener.
After deleting these sounds, some of the techniques used in lossless encoding can also be applied,
further shrinking ﬁle size. The signal stored from a lossy codec is not identical to the original
uncompressed ﬁle. For this reason Evan Ruzanski and other audio researchers have long
questioned whether lossy ﬁles are of as good quality as lossless ﬁles, and whether people can in
fact discern a difference (43). As with the other two methods, there is a range of codecs used to
compress audio in a lossy manner. According to B. Grill and S. Quackenbush, the authors of the
ofﬁcial documentation for the MPEG-1 audio codecs, the best known lossy audio codec is the
MP3 codec which is short for MPEG layer 3 encoding. Other codecs include Apple’s AAC
encoding scheme distributed through iTunes and the Xiph Foundation’s Ogg Vorbis format used
by the Spotify streaming service.
