important, because it is essential that a low-power device such as a smart phone be able to decode

comparison of lossless audio codecs, the speed and energy used for this process varies greatly
between different lossless codecs (5-6). Speciﬁcally, van Beurden’s comparison ﬁnds that the
FLAC format is one of the fastest lossless codecs both to encode and to decode (5-6). In
comparison, Monkey’s audio (APE) takes much more time and CPU power to encode and decode
(van Beurden 5-6). ALAC encodes and decodes at a rate between that of FLAC and that of APE
(van Beurden, 5-6).
Decoding speeds are important when playing compressed music ﬁles, but in order to be
played at all a codec must be compatible with range of devices. Robertson calls MP3 the
”Granddaddy of compressed internet audio,” and says that MP3 has almost universal
compatibility (59). Simon Den Uijl Henk de Vries and Deniz Bayramoglu call MP3 the ”market
standard” suggesting that no format has greater compatibility than MP3 (1). Almost any device
can play an MP3 ﬁle regardless of its form factor or manufacturer. In contrast to this, both Apple
formats (AAC and ALAC) have historically only been playable on Apple devices. More recently,
however, Apple has released its codecs for free public use, and perhaps greater compatibility will
follow. Ogg Vorbis is not very well known, however Ian Corbett says it is gaining popularity and
compatibility through Spotify. While few device makers natively support Ogg Vorbis, Ogg Vorbis
ﬁles can be played from a wide range of devices using the Spotify app. FLAC, the other open
source codec from the Xiph Foundation has quickly become a cross-platform standard for lossless
audio compression. According to the ofﬁcial FLAC documentation the format is currently
compatible with at least 45 different brands of home stereo, car audio and mobile audio players
(Coalson). FLAC ﬁles are also distributed from a growing number of websites and apps such as
Tidal, Beatport and HD Tracks.
