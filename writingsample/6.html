Why is it that these sites choose to distribute a format which produces larger ﬁle sizes and
is less standard than a simple MP3? The answer lies in music quality. While the algorithms used
to encode lossy formats are good, they are not perfect. Especially at very low bitrates, the
difference in music quality between uncompressed and lossy audio becomes clear. How
noticeable is this difference in quality? Listening tests directly verify the subjective quality of
compressed music. While listening tests take signiﬁcant money and time to conduct, they have
also identiﬁed certain quirks in the perceived quality of music. One of these quirks, found by
Ruzanski, is that certain genres of music can actually sound better to listeners when compressed
in a lossy manner at a high bit rate than when not compressed at all (45). Dermot Cambell,
Edward Jones and Martin Glavin, researchers in audio quality assessment techniques, say that as
a faster, lower cost alternative to listening tests, an algorithm called PEAQ can be utilized (1490).
This algorithm uses a psychoacoustic model and a cognitive model to compare the perceived
audio ﬁdelity of compressed audio signals (Cambell, Jones and Glavin 1490). While PEAQ can
be a useful tool, it is not a perfect model of human hearing (Cambell, Jones, Glavin 1490), and a
listening test represents more truly how people perceive quality. In a listening test, Ruzanski
found that college aged students could only notice a difference between 128kbps MP3 and
uncompressed audio in two genres of music: dynamic classical and rap (45). While the study is
not complete having only surveyed college aged students, Ruzanski points out that it is known
that as people age they lose high frequency hearing (45). This means that a sample including
older people would only further solidify Ruzanski’s ﬁndings (45). Sebastian Mares conducted a
listening test and found that for a given bit rate, AAC sounds better than MP3, and Ogg Vorbis
sounds marginally better than AAC. Hines performed a comprehensive listening test across three
different listening devices and found that on consumer headphones listeners could not tell the
difference between 256kbps AAC and the reference, uncompressed recording (1175).
